📖 What is a Lunar Calendar?

A lunar calendar is a calendar based on the monthly cycles of the Moon's phases (synodic months), in contrast to solar calendars, whose annual cycles are based on the solar year. Since each lunation is approximately 29.5 days, it is common for the months of a lunar calendar to alternate between 29 and 30 days. A lunar year of 12 such lunations is 354 days, 8 hours, 48 minutes, 34 seconds — about 11 to 12 days shorter than the solar year.[reference:0][reference:1]

🌿 Origins: Who invented the lunar calendar

Scholars have argued that ancient hunters conducted regular astronomical observations of the Moon back in the Upper Palaeolithic. Samuel L. Macey dates the earliest uses of the Moon as a time-measuring device back to 28,000–30,000 years ago.[reference:2] The Sumerians were probably the first to develop a calendar based entirely on the recurrence of lunar phases. Each Sumero-Babylonian month began on the first day of visibility of the new Moon.[reference:3] The lunisolar calendar descends from an older Sumerian calendar used in the 4th and 3rd millennia BCE.[reference:4]

🏛 Who is the authority today

Modern scientific calculation of lunar phases builds upon the work of polymath Johann Heinrich Lambert (1728–1777), who made important contributions to astronomy, photometry, and mathematics. A crater on the Moon is named after him.[reference:5][reference:6] The most precise ephemerides today are published by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL). The Swiss Ephemeris — used by our main test — is a compressed version of the JPL DE431 ephemeris, covering the time range 13,000 BCE to 17,000 CE.[reference:7][reference:8]

🌎 The Moon Today: From Myths to Precision

Though often linked with astrology and folklore, the lunar calendar's astronomical basis remains strictly scientific. Today, lunar cycles are studied for agriculture, fishing, medical research, and even stock trading. The most widely observed pure lunar calendar today is the Islamic calendar.[reference:9]
